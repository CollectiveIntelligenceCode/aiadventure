There is a pattern to the releases that matter. They are not always the biggest models. They are not always the ones that score highest on standard benchmarks. But they share something harder to quantify: they change the calculus of what people believe is possible.

## The pattern of inflection

When GPT-3 landed in 2020, the conversation shifted from "language models are a research curiosity" to "language models could be a product." That shift did not happen because of a benchmark number. It happened because people tried it and immediately started imagining applications they had never considered before.

The same thing happened with Stable Diffusion's open release. With GPT-4's multimodal capabilities. With AlphaFold. In each case, there was a before and an after — a moment when the field's shared mental model updated, and the space of considered possibilities expanded.

## What makes a model an inflection point

Three things tend to distinguish a model that matters from one that doesn't, despite similar capability scores.

First: it crosses a threshold of reliability on something that was previously unreliable. Not marginally better — reliably good enough to use in the real world. Second: it makes something genuinely new feel tractable. Not just faster or cheaper to do what was already being done. A new category of thing becomes possible. Third: it is accessible. A model that only a handful of researchers can use does not change the field's direction the way a deployed, available system does.

## What the current moment is telling us

The pattern is repeating, but the implications are wider now. The models being released today are not just changing what applications are possible — they are beginning to change how the work of building applications is done.

That is a different kind of inflection point. Not just a better tool. A tool that modifies the conditions of its own development.

The question worth asking now is not "how good is this model?" It is "what does this make possible that wasn't tractable before?" The answers to that question are more interesting, and more consequential, than any benchmark.
